# The Sepsis Chain of Survival: A Comprehensive Framework for Improving Sepsis Outcomes

ABSTRACT: The “Chain of Survival” concept, developed for cardiac arrest, emphasizes a seamless and interconnected set, of time-critical interventions that are not strictly linear, to improve survival. This paradigm of urgent recognition and response to life-threatening conditions has evolved to apply to acute medical conditions, including sepsis. The “Sepsis Chain of Survival” underscores the importance of early recognition, prompt emergency medical services activation, timely antimicrobial administration and appropriate fluid resuscitation, optimized critical care management, effective source control and infection management, and comprehensive post-sepsis care. By adopting this approach, healthcare systems can improve sepsis outcomes through a coordinated, multifaceted strategy. This model highlights the critical role of public and healthcare worker awareness, education, community response, and continuous monitoring. Addressing the importance and interdependence of each link, this framework aims to improve survival rates and patient recovery by ensuring timely and effective sepsis management across diverse resource settings in infants, children, and adults. 

KEYWORDS: chain of survival; critical care management; early recognition; emergency response; post-sepsis care; sepsis 

The “Chain of Survival” concept refers to interdependent interventions that must occur to optimize survival from life-threatening conditions that require seamless time-critical sequenced interventions, such as sudden cardiac arrest, stroke, severe trauma, choking, anaphylaxis, maternal hemorrhage, and newborn asphyxia (1). All “Chains of Survival,” inside or outside hospital settings, begin with awareness, recognition and early access to care, in which first actions are delivered as quickly as possible (1–3). Early access includes emergency system activation to get trained responder input and movement to the scene (2). The concept of the “Chain of Survival” has evolved over several decades, with links to the chain added to incorporate prevention, rehabilitation and recovery. Identifying and implementing effective health system interventions are crucial for better outcomes for sepsis patients across resource variable settings. This paradigm of urgent recognition and response to life-threatening conditions has evolved to apply to other acute medical conditions including sepsis.

The chain of survival is now being applied to many life-threatening conditions that require time-critical interventions. Sepsis, as defined by Sepsis-3, is lifethreatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection (7). Sepsis can develop both in the community and within hospital settings. Regardless of where sepsis originates, early recognition, and prompt system activation is vital, which includes Emergency Medical Services (EMS) telephone dispatcher in the community and rapid response protocols in the hospital. The growing recognition of the benefits of conceptualizing sepsis management along a continuum of care in a “Chain of Survival” enables healthcare systems to better address the complexities of sepsis care. For this reason, the time has come to apply the chain of survival concept to sepsis (8, 9). 

# SEPSIS CHAIN OF SURVIVAL

The Sepsis Chain of Survival describes a systematic networked approach to improve outcomes for patients of all ages with sepsis (10). It depicts crucial steps including awareness, early recognition, prompt intervention, and comprehensive management throughout the continuum of care; weakness at any point in the link lessens the chance of survival with a good quality of life (11). The steps in the link are interdependent but they are not necessarily applied in a linear sequence but all 

must be taken into consideration for best chances of survival in sepsis. 

The infographic (Fig. 1) is a simple way to educate the public and healthcare workers (12). It illustrates that each link is critical and interdependent (in a sequence and not always linear), and the Chain of Survival is only as strong as its weakest link (12). 

# Awareness and Prevention

Awareness is the first step of early action to prevent the progression of infection to sepsis or septic shock and focuses on community and healthcare workforce education to improve recognition of early signs of infection and sepsis and to promote timely care-seeking behavior and targeted intervention (13). 

Preventing the infections that can precipitate sepsis is critical and consists of two aspects: 1) prevention of infections through implementing public health programs promoting equitable vaccination of vulnerable populations against sepsis-associated infections, Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene programs, which improve access to clean water, sanitation facilities, and hygiene practices (14), and infection prevention and control within healthcare settings encompassing stringent hand hygiene practices, appropriate disinfection and sterilization protocols, and meticulous surgical site infection prevention and 2) preventing progression to sepsis in the healthcare setting through timely recognition and management of patients with infections at risk of deterioration. 

Ongoing efforts are needed to educate the public, healthcare providers, and caregivers about the signs and symptoms of early sepsis and preventive measures.

# Early Recognition and Call for Help

Recognition and timely activation of care focuses on detecting early signs of sepsis and promptly activating appropriate care pathways across all settings, from community and pre-hospital environments to healthcare facilities. 

Community members and healthcare providers need to promptly recognize sepsis and take action from signs of infection, such as fever, elevated heart rate, rapid breathing, and altered mental status (15). Promoting awareness of “danger signs” to recognize potential sepsis is a valuable approach. The World Health Organization’s (WHO) Emergency Triage Assessment and Treatment guidelines have been effective in helping healthcare and community workers identify children in need of urgent care and may have utility in early recognition of sepsis. While not specific to sepsis alone, these danger signs can serve as important indicators that prompt action to further evaluate for sepsis or other severe conditions (16). By harnessing the potential of existing frameworks and promoting awareness of “danger signs” among healthcare workers and the public, early recognition of sepsis and life-threatening critical conditions can be improved. This, in turn, can lead to better patient outcomes through implementation of timely intervention (17). 

Training of community pre-hospital responders to recognize sepsis and initiate protocolized management with oxygen administration, appropriate fluid resuscitation, and antimicrobial administration upon arrival at the scene and during transport is essential (13, 18). Further ensuring safe and timely transport from the scene to the closest medical facility is imperative. In many low-resource settings (LRS), timely EMS responders may not be available, and community responders often function in this role until help arrives. 

Prediction through early warning algorithms or automated tools implemented in electronic health records are promising approaches within hospital and ICU settings, where enabling technologies can be integrated into clinical workflows to enhance early detection and intervention (19). Early prediction, monitoring, and screening for deterioration, digital triage tools, and application of sepsis bundles of care including escalation protocols allow for early recognition and timely targeted interventions that can prevent progression to life-threatening organ dysfunction (13). 

# Continuous Source Control

Rapidly identify the source of infection and treat accordingly. Timely surgical drainage, debridement, or removal of infected devices (e.g., IV catheters, prosthetic devices) is necessary to eliminate the source of infection should be initiated as soon as it is feasible and safe alongside antimicrobial therapy and supportive care (20, 21). Source control should be a continuous exercise where necessary. 

# Appropriate Fluids, Oxygen, and Antibiotics

Early antimicrobial therapy (ideally within the first hour), oxygen when required, and appropriate fluid resuscitation are foundational components of sepsis management. Early administration of appropriate and available antimicrobials that target the suspected causative organism (bacteria, virus, fungus, etc.) should be informed by local data on infection trends and susceptibility or resistance (7, 15, 22, 23). Identify conditions that may require alternative management, such as dengue fever or malaria with fever and severe anemia. Monitor the patient’s response to treatment closely, adjust therapies as needed based on patient response and organ dysfunction and narrow antimicrobial coverage once a definitive organism is identified. 

Fluid resuscitation is a critical component of sepsis management in high-resource settings, where monitoring and adjunctive supportive therapies are available to manage consequences of any excess fluid administration. In LRS, where such supportive measures may not be available, fluid resuscitation should be approached with caution, and alternative strategies may be required (24, 25). Specifically, considerations for fluid overload are particularly important in conditions such as acute respiratory distress syndrome, severe anemia/malaria, impaired perfusion in children with fever, severe malnutrition, and dengue fever, and appropriate guidance should be followed (26). Timely and appropriate administration of supplemental oxygen, where indicated by hypoxia or signs of respiratory distress, can improve tissue oxygenation, support cellular function and mitigate risk of organ dysfunction (14, 27). 

# Critical Care Without Walls

Early initiation and critical care management across the continuum of care should be provided, with 

appropriate monitoring/technology based on resource availability (23). A coordinated effort across the entire healthcare continuum, including both hospital-based and pre-hospital care providers, is important. While much expertise resides within hospitals, collaboration with community healthcare workers, EMS, and clinics/referral centers is crucial to ensuring seamless care from the community to the hospital setting. Interdisciplinary providers should work together to accomplish a Sepsis Chain of Survival. 

Care may need to be provided in a variety of settings, based on need and resources. The ICU “without walls” concept allows for flexibility and provision of the highest-quality care possible (28). It denotes provision of time-critical interventions using available resources in any healthcare setting even when ICU is not available. Patients exhibiting signs of worsening sepsis or septic shock require prompt transfer to a critical care capable environment, to facilitate aggressive resuscitation, close monitoring, and delivery of specialized care (29). Vital signs, laboratory values, and organ function should be monitored closely. Organ support measures, including mechanical ventilation, vasopressor administration, blood products, and renal replacement therapy, should be considered in the context of the patient’s clinical status and resource considerations. 

# Post-Sepsis Care and Return to Community

Post-sepsis care focuses on supporting survivors’ recovery and reintegration into the community. Key interventions include screening for economic and social challenges that may hinder recovery, providing written and verbal sepsis education to empower patients and caregivers, and referring survivors to peer support groups and psychosocial resources. These measures address the physical, cognitive, and emotional consequences of sepsis, including post-intensive care syndrome (PICS), and align with the recommendations of the Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021 guidelines (15). Sepsis survivors are at risk for PICS, a condition that encompasses a range of physical, cognitive, and emotional difficulties. PICS can manifest as physical limitations, such as muscle weakness and fatigue, cognitive impairments including memory and attention deficits, and emotional challenges like anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder. Physical limitations associated with PICS often include 

persistent muscle weakness, joint stiffness, and chronic pain, which can significantly impact a patient’s ability to perform daily activities and reduce their overall quality of life (30, 31). Rehabilitation strategies aimed to improve physical function are essential components of post-sepsis care. Early mobility may have potential benefits in sepsis recovery; however, current evidence is mixed (30, 31). Post-sepsis care and follow-up are crucial for survivors and their families. Comprehensive post-sepsis care includes proper nutrition, physical rehabilitation, psychologic support, and management of sequelae such as post-sepsis syndrome (persistent physical symptoms or psychologic challenges). Clinics that focus on the post-hospital needs of patients who have been critically ill can facilitate recovery (32, 33). Primary healthcare providers should be familiar with the needs of a post-ICU patient and tailor the frequency and nature of visits to the patient/family needs and available resources. Healthcare personnel and family members should monitor for complications such as recurrence of infection, organ dysfunction, or physiologic sequelae and be empowered to actively aid in recovery. Knowledgeable family members and caregivers can be important advocates for communitybased education on recognizing and managing sepsis in the community. Critically ill patients who have been in the ICU may have psychologic problems such as mood changes, difficulty sleeping, altered cognition, and anxiety/depression as a part of the post-ICU syndrome. Recognition, counseling, and psychologic support are needed for patients and families or caregivers. This support helps in coping with the emotional and psychologic effects of the situation, reduces stress, and promotes overall well-being. Activities could include debriefing sessions, access to mental health resources, and ongoing support, as needed, but should be contextualized to the system, the individual, and the resources. 

# CALL TO ACTION

The Sepsis Chain of Survival provides a structured framework that simplifies and organizes existing best practices into a cohesive and actionable interdependent interventions (8, 15). This framework is designed to be implemented across the continuum of care, encompassing both community and in-hospital locations. The steps are not necessarily linear and do not have to be implemented in sequence, but all steps should be 

considered in a systematic approach to management of patients with sepsis. It emphasizes the importance of engaging both healthcare systems and communities in the early recognition and effective management of sepsis. The Sepsis Chain of Survival is a “call to action” for healthcare systems worldwide to adopt an integrated, systematic approach to sepsis management for patients of all ages, contextualized at the local level. Promotion of community and healthcare provider awareness and action promotes response to the challenge of treating life-threatening sepsis. The concept of the Sepsis Chain of Survival highlights the necessity of a cohesive and coordinated effort, encouraging optimization of each link, highlighting interdependence of the steps necessary to improve survival and patient/ family quality of life outcomes across resource variable settings. 

The implementation of a Sepsis Chain of Survival requires a coordinated effort across diverse community and healthcare settings. The following actions are identified: 

• Public Awareness Campaigns: Increase public awareness about what sepsis is, and the importance of early recognition and prompt medical attention. 

• Healthcare Provider Training: Enhance training programs for healthcare providers and emergency responders on the early recognition and context-specific management of sepsis. 

• Standardized Protocols: Establish standardized, contextappropriate protocols for sepsis management that include prediction, prevention, early recognition, and comprehensive coordinated and team-based care. 

• Resource Allocation: Ensure necessary tools and support are provided to implement the Sepsis Chain of Survival effectively across resource variable settings. 

• Health Systems Strengthening: In LRS, the availability of essential treatments such as antibiotics, fluids, and oxygen is important. 

Continuous Audit, Monitoring, and Evaluation: Regularly monitor and evaluate the implementation of the Sepsis Chain of Survival to identify areas for improvement and ensure optimal patient outcomes. 

By integrated actions in a Sepsis Chain of Survival, patient survival and recovery can be enhanced, ultimately improving outcomes for sepsis patients globally. 

# CONCLUSIONS

The Sepsis Chain of Survival concept represents a comprehensive and systematic approach to improve 

outcomes for patients with suspected sepsis, recognizing that timely and coordinated interventions are crucial to manage this life-threatening condition. By extending and contextualizing the well-established principles of the Sudden Cardiac Arrest Chain of Survival model to sepsis management, patient survival and recovery can be enhanced across diverse and global settings, and it can be adapted based on resource availability. Sepsis Chain of Survival does not aim to replace advanced sepsis care but to complement it by establishing a foundation of care that is accessible, actionable, and adaptable. 

# FUTURE DIRECTIONS

Robust coordination and governance of sepsis programs should incorporate key components such as the Sepsis Chain of Survival. Alignment with international consensus on adult and pediatric sepsis definitions and guidelines, and adapting to various resource contexts, is desirable (7, 8, 34). Improving resuscitation systems, provider training, and fostering a culture of excellence among healthcare professionals is crucial. Emerging technology such as low bandwidth telecommunication, telemedicine, artificial intelligence, detection, and novel diagnostic and treatment tools hold much anticipated promise to enhance sepsis outcomes (35). 

Continued education, community involvement, and a holistic approach will enhance patient outcomes worldwide. Several international initiatives have been developed to improve the standardization and implementation of best practices in sepsis care. For example, the WHO’s Emergency Care Systems framework aims to strengthen emergency care in resource-limited settings. Engagement of funders, Ministries of Health, and the WHO is vital for the successful establishment of sepsis as the tracer disease for tracking and reporting system responses to life-threatening diseases. This coordinated effort ensures a comprehensive and robust response to sepsis and other critical health threats (36).
